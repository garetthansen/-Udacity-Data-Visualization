Exploring Annual Percentage Rate (APR)¶

by Garett Hansen¶

Investigation Overview¶

For this analysis, I chose to investigate several variables that I felt could potentially impact a borrower's Annual Percentage Rate (APR) for better or worse. These variables are income range, credit score (and rating), and loan size.

Dataset Overview¶

The provided data is from marketplace lending company Prosper and is comprised of 113,937 individual loans across a wide range of loan types (i.e. home improvement, auto, student, etc). While 81 different variables are attributed to each loan, only the subset of variables listed previously were analyzed.

Distribution of APR¶

Borrower rates span a wide range of possible values, hinting that the rate applied to a loan can widely vary borrower to borrower. The rates here take on a nearly unimodal distribution, with the notable exception around the 36% area.

APR vs Income Range and Credit Rating¶

When factoring annual income, those with a favorable credit rating consistently experienced a lower APR than those with less favorable credit ratings; howeve, while those with good, very good, or exceptional ratings experienced a lower APR as income increased, those with fair or very poor credit ratings experienced slightly similar or occasionally slightly higher APR as income increased.

APR vs Income Range and Loan Size¶

Uemployed borrowers experience some of the highest APR rates, regardless of loan size. Very small loans (1000-4000) experience the highest rates for those with a provided income, although the rates do rise and lower slightly depending on income range. Small and medium-sized loans experience very similar APR rates for those with reported income and are nearly identical for those earning above 50,0000. Perhaps unsurprisingly, those with high incomes and large loan sizes enjoy some of the lowest rates.
